Daily living assistance: from groceries to hands-on care

Support needs often change task by task. Understanding IADLs and ADLs can help a household describe what is difficult, compare service options, and preserve choice where possible.

By the Aging Gracefully Editorial Team · Published July 9, 2026 · Reviewed July 20, 2026

How to use this guide: This is general education, not individualized medical, legal, financial, or safety advice. Confirm coverage, eligibility, and care decisions with the relevant plan, agency, or qualified professional.

IADLs: the household layer

Instrumental Activities of Daily Living are tasks used to manage a household and community life, such as shopping, meal preparation, housekeeping, laundry, transportation, and appointments. Support can be limited to the tasks a person wants help with. Review changes such as unpaid bills, spoiled food, missed appointments, or unsafe home maintenance with the person rather than assuming they require a particular level of care.

ADLs: the personal care layer

Basic Activities of Daily Living include bathing, dressing, grooming, toileting, transferring, and eating. Support may come from family, privately hired workers, home-care agencies, or eligible public and insurance programs. Agency screening, supervision, insurance, backup staffing, minimum visits, and training vary, so ask each provider to explain them in writing. Verify benefit eligibility directly with the insurer or government program.

Nutrition without the cooking

When shopping or cooking becomes difficult, options may include grocery delivery, shared meal preparation, local senior nutrition programs, or commercial prepared meals. Availability, cost, delivery schedules, and eligibility differ by location. A meal marketed for a medical condition is not automatically appropriate for an individual; discuss dietary restrictions with a qualified clinician or dietitian.

Making the house do more of the work

Adaptive equipment may make particular tasks easier, but fit and safe use matter. Examples include long-handled reachers, dressing aids, shower seating, handheld sprayers, and easy-grip kitchen tools. An occupational therapist can assess tasks and suggest options. Insurance coverage depends on medical necessity, plan rules, and the item or service involved.

Common questions

How much does a home care aide cost?

Rates vary by location, worker qualifications, agency model, schedule, visit minimums, and level of assistance. Request a written rate sheet that explains minimum hours, overtime, cancellation fees, transportation charges, and rate changes rather than relying on a national estimate.

Does Medicare pay for help with bathing and dressing?

Medicare may cover limited home-health aide services when the person is also receiving qualifying skilled home-health care and meets the program’s rules. It generally does not cover ongoing custodial care by itself. Verify the current rules with Medicare and the care agency.

How can we discuss bringing in help?

Ask which tasks are frustrating and what kind of help would feel acceptable. Discuss privacy, schedule, cost, and who chooses the worker. Starting with a specific agreed task can make it easier to evaluate whether the arrangement is useful.
