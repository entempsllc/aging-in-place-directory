Staying connected, engaged, and sharp

Social isolation and loneliness are associated with health risks. A practical connection plan can combine relationships, community activities, transportation, accessible technology, and professional support when needed.

By the Aging Gracefully Editorial Team · Published July 9, 2026 · Reviewed July 20, 2026

How to use this guide: This is general education, not individualized medical, legal, financial, or safety advice. Confirm coverage, eligibility, and care decisions with the relevant plan, agency, or qualified professional.

Companionship on the calendar

Ask the person which relationships and activities matter to them, then make the next step specific: a recurring call, a ride to a group, a neighbor visit, or an accessible online activity. Companion-care services and friendly-visitor programs may be options when family and friends are far away. Confirm worker screening, supervision, duties, fees, and boundaries with the provider.

Senior centers and adult day programs

Senior centers may offer meals, classes, exercise, recreation, and referrals for adults who participate independently. Adult day programs provide scheduled, supervised services, but staffing, clinical support, licensing, eligibility, and transportation vary by program and state. Tour the program, review the daily schedule and emergency procedures, and ask how it matches the participant’s needs.

Cognitive stimulation that isn't a chore

Choose activities the person enjoys and can access rather than promising that a particular game or class will prevent cognitive decline. Options might include books, music, games, art, language learning, faith communities, volunteering, or intergenerational projects. Libraries and community organizations may offer large-print materials, audiobooks, delivery, or adaptive equipment; local availability varies.

Technology that closes distance

Technology can help when it matches the person’s preferences, hearing, vision, dexterity, internet access, and privacy expectations. Compare the number of steps required, captioning and volume controls, contact approval, account recovery, security updates, and whether remote support is available. Practice together, but obtain consent before enabling auto-answer, cameras, or remote administration.

Common questions

What's the difference between a senior center and an adult day program?

Senior centers generally offer community activities and services for adults who can participate with limited support. Adult day programs provide scheduled supervision and may offer personal care, health services, or memory support. Models and licensing vary, so verify the specific program.

How much does companion care cost?

Rates and visit minimums vary by location, provider, schedule, and worker qualifications. Some community organizations offer volunteer friendly-visiting programs. Request written fees and service terms, and use the Eldercare Locator to identify local options.

What if someone dislikes age-labeled programs?

Start with their interests and goals rather than the program label. Compare a specific class, meal, volunteer role, or group, and invite the person to review or visit options before deciding.
