Getting around after the car keys

A transportation plan can preserve access to healthcare, groceries, work, community activities, and relationships when driving changes or stops.

By the Aging Gracefully Editorial Team · Published July 9, 2026 · Reviewed July 20, 2026

How to use this guide: This is general education, not individualized medical, legal, financial, or safety advice. Confirm coverage, eligibility, and care decisions with the relevant plan, agency, or qualified professional.

Medical transport

Start by asking the health plan, clinic, and local transit agency what transportation is available. Some Medicare Advantage plans offer supplemental rides. State Medicaid programs arrange non-emergency medical transportation for eligible beneficiaries under program rules that vary by state. Where fixed-route public transit exists, complementary ADA paratransit serves eligible riders whose disabilities prevent use of the regular service; application and reservation procedures are local.

Everyday errands

Local options may include volunteer-driver programs, senior vans, public transit, taxi vouchers, or commercial rideshare and concierge services. Compare service area, advance notice, accessibility, escort policies, wait time, cancellation fees, payment method, and whether the rider must use a smartphone. The Eldercare Locator transportation page is a starting point for local programs.

The walkability audit

If walking is part of the plan, review the actual route at the time it will be used. Look for surface damage, curb ramps, crossing time, lighting, traffic, weather exposure, rest points, and access to help. Report hazards through the local public-works or accessibility process. A mobility aid should be selected and fitted with qualified guidance when balance, strength, or gait is a concern.

Planning the driving retirement

Driving decisions should consider vision, cognition, movement, medications, crash or near-miss history, and state licensing rules. A clinician or driver-rehabilitation specialist can help evaluate ability and possible adaptations. Test alternative transportation before it is urgently needed, and use the NHTSA older-driver resources to structure the conversation.

Common questions

Who may qualify for complementary ADA paratransit?

Eligibility is based on whether a disability prevents a person from using the fixed-route transit system for some or all trips—not simply on age or a diagnosis. Apply through the local transit agency and follow its documentation process.

Are rides available without a smartphone?

Some transit agencies, volunteer programs, taxi services, and commercial concierge services accept reservations by phone. Availability and fees vary, so confirm who operates the ride, how drivers are screened, what assistance is offered, and how cancellations are handled.

What do volunteer-driver programs cost?

Programs set their own fares, donations, eligibility, service areas, and reservation rules. Contact the local program directly or use the federal Eldercare Locator to identify options to compare.
