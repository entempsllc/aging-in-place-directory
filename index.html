A quick family guide to aging-in-place modifications

Reviewed July 20, 2026 · See how we research and update content.

Aging in place means remaining in your home and community as you grow older. The right plan depends on the person, the house, available support, and budget. Start with the task that is difficult today, then consider what may change over the next few years.

What changes the price

  • Scope: a surface-mounted accessory is different from work requiring framing, plumbing, electrical changes, or structural alterations.
  • Fit: stair shape, doorway width, ramp rise, and the user’s movement needs affect equipment and labor.
  • Permits and finish work: inspections, waterproofing, tile repair, painting, and disposal may or may not be included.
  • Product and warranty: compare model numbers, weight limits, parts coverage, labor coverage, and local service response.

For larger work, get at least two written, itemized proposals and compare the included scope rather than relying on a national price range.

Who may help pay

Coverage and eligibility vary. Original Medicare generally does not pay for permanent home modifications, although it may cover certain prescribed durable medical equipment. Some Medicare Advantage plans offer supplemental home-safety benefits; check the plan’s current Evidence of Coverage. Medicaid home- and community-based services and VA housing grants may help eligible households. Start with the federal Eldercare Locator, Medicaid HCBS information, and VA disability housing grants, then verify current rules directly with the program.

What "CAPS" means

A Certified Aging-in-Place Specialist has completed National Association of Home Builders training related to serving older adults and adapting homes. The credential can be one useful qualification, but it is not a substitute for checking the contractor’s relevant license, insurance, references, and project experience. Learn more from the official NAHB CAPS overview.

Common questions

Should I hire a handyman or a specialist?

Match the provider to the work. Even a small grab-bar installation requires appropriate anchors and knowledge of the wall assembly. Plumbing, electrical, structural, waterproofing, ramp, and lift work may require licensed trades or permits. Ask the local permitting authority what applies before signing a contract.

When should we start modifying the home?

Plan before an urgent event forces a rushed decision. Start with a walkthrough of tasks that are already difficult, near misses, and likely changes in mobility. An occupational therapist or other qualified professional can help when needs are complex.

Do these changes affect resale value?

Resale effects vary by market, design, workmanship, and the type of modification. Ask whether equipment can be removed without major repairs and choose changes that work for a broad range of users when practical. A local real-estate professional can discuss market-specific considerations.

Home-safety products: what to evaluate before buying

Some small improvements can be handled without remodeling, but installation and fit still matter. Use these examples to prepare questions before choosing a product.

Suction & screw-mount grab bars

Screw-mount bars anchored into studs are the safe choice; suction bars are for balance assistance only, never full weight.

Read aging-at-home guidance

Non-slip bath mats & strips

The cheapest fall-prevention upgrade in the house. Look for textured, mildew-resistant surfaces.

Read fall-prevention guidance

Raised toilet seats

An instant comfort-height upgrade with no plumbing work, from around $30.

Read home-safety guidance

Motion-sensor night lights

Light the path from bed to bathroom automatically — nighttime trips are a top fall trigger.

Read fall-prevention guidance

Product categories are provided as planning examples, not endorsements. Compare safety features, return policies, installation requirements, and advice from a qualified professional before buying.
