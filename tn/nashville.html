Planning an aging-in-place project in Nashville

Start with the person’s daily routine and the specific barriers in the home—not a product catalog. For bathroom or entry work, ask each provider for a written scope that covers wall blocking or anchors, slip resistance, clearances, drainage, warranties, and who is responsible for permits. For stairs or larger remodels, compare an equipment-only quote with a whole-home accessibility assessment.

Check licensing and permits

Use the Tennessee Board for Licensing Contractors to review state requirements and contractor information. Before work begins, check Metro Nashville’s construction and permit guidance to learn whether the project requires a permit or inspection. A CAPS designation reflects aging-in-place training; it does not replace any license, insurance, or permit required for the work.

Look for local aging and disability support

The Tennessee Department of Disability and Aging publishes program and contact information for older adults and people with disabilities. Eligibility and covered modifications vary, so confirm current terms directly with the program or health plan before relying on funding.

Compare Nashville estimates carefully

Prices vary with structure, product, labor, electrical or plumbing changes, permit needs, and finish work. Get at least two itemized estimates, verify insurance, ask for recent comparable references, and avoid paying the entire project cost before work is completed.

Five checks before hiring

  1. Confirm the company serves your address and has experience with the specific modification you need.
  2. Verify licenses, registrations, insurance, and permit requirements with the appropriate state or local authority.
  3. Request a written, itemized scope covering labor, materials, permits, warranty terms, payment schedule, and change orders.
  4. Check recent references for comparable accessibility or aging-in-place work. A high online rating alone is not proof of relevant experience.
  5. For larger accessibility projects, ask who will assess fit and function and whether a CAPS professional, occupational therapist, or other qualified specialist should be involved.

Official starting points: the FTC home-improvement scam guide, the federal Eldercare Locator, and the NAHB explanation of the CAPS credential.

Frequently asked questions — Nashville

How do I screen a contractor serving Nashville?

Confirm that the business serves your address and has completed comparable accessibility work. Check required state or local licenses, registrations, insurance, permits, recent references, written warranty terms, and complaint information with the appropriate public authority. A directory listing or online rating is not a substitute for those checks.

What should I ask before a grab-bar installation?

Ask how the installer will identify framing or approved backing, choose fasteners for the wall assembly, protect waterproofing, confirm placement for the user, and handle repairs. Timing depends on the number of bars, wall construction, access, and whether blocking or finish work is required.

How do I compare a walk-in tub and a curbless shower?

Compare transfer method, standing and seated use, caregiver access, fill and drain time, slip resistance, door clearance, waterproofing, plumbing capacity, maintenance, and emergency exit. The right choice depends on the user and the room; consider an occupational-therapy assessment when mobility needs are complex.
